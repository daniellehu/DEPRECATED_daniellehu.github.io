Best Enjoyed By - this particular podcast spoke about the design of those printed labels on everyday produce. I think the important takeaway here is that you can't always know when a food will expire. Calm down a bit next time your milk or bread says something like that, and just know that no one knows exactly when something will "expire." Expire as in taste bad won't necessarily kill you, but make sure that those dairy products that you get don't taste bad AND kill you.

Bone Music - This one was really cool. So during the USSR's reign, propaganda from the west was severely blocked right? Soviets were shielded from any culture that promoted non-communist ideas. Well because of this, music in particular was fancied by a lot of people who lived in the Soviet Union. They wanted access to Jazz and Rock records, but obviously couldn't because they were illegal. Well some guy decided to create some of these records, but getting the legit material for a vinyl disc was very difficult there. So instead, he used these exposed X-Ray scans to encode the music. This lasted longer and was easy to access (i.e. hospitals and medical archives). When these so-called "bone music" became popular, private household kitchens became cultural hot spots for free expression and exchange of ideas. Kinda like in the enlightenment when they had Barbershops. These records would be sold by guys in trenchcoats on the street. The KGB caught on and would send out fake vinyl records that would break players, and cracked down on more and more people.

Tube benders - Neon lights! There was a time when they were only for luxurious places. Eventually, when the lights died out, they would be expensive to up-keep. Many became upgraded to LED lights and therefore Neon Lights started to die out. The flickering neon light started to become a metaphor for loneliness. Now there's a slight comeback for keeping Neon lights alive, but really... things have changed over time.

Pagodas and Dragon Gates - Chinatown and Chinese people... much relevant to me. There was a time when Chinese people were extremely discriminated against. I'm talking the Chinese Exclusion Act.... I'm talking when there was an earthquake that destroyed San Francisco, Chinatown was not saved due to reasons of white superiority. They instead blew up houses in Chinatown that caught fire after the Earthquake in an effort to stop the fire from spreading. In post-earthquake settings, the city refused to rebuild Chinatown. They wanted to instead relocate those that lost homes, and to rebuild a new place that was more western and "perfect" (WASPS). The Chinese were able to counter this relocation by threatening (in a very peaceful manner) to relocate their Chinese trade connections as well. The city stopped their racism and basically allowed them to rebuild. What the Chinese decided to do with this opportunity to rebuild was great. They hired a white architect and a white engineer. Both didn't know much about China and its culture/traditions. So what they did, was create a very westernized, stereotyped/idealized view of China. These views were clean and non-threatening, so instead of having a thug/Triad type of image, they started to just become very "Chinese" (ching-chong) like. Westerners were attracted to this idealized version and revenue for this tiny little town grew. Win-Win (sort of not really cause it's still kind of discriminatory for Chinese people).

Worst Smell in the World"Liquid Ass" - something that smells really bad, but was useful to help soldiers practice real-life simulated situations for Iraq.

Fixing the Hobo Suit - Designing the costumes for superheroes. Make them modern, yet not offensive to the original comic strips. Superman's red underwear? Not cool anymore so let's just make them blue. Let's also put chain-mail under his costume to seem shiny and strong. That kind of stuff. Kind of cool, but not that much for me personally.

The Landlord's Game - Monopoly. Was actually originally created to make people not want capitalism/monopolies. Wanted to promote another type of tax system, but instead made people like the game. The game was first idealized with "Landlord's Game" which didn't have much visuals, but the idea was there. Then a guy took that idea and developed it for himself by adding better visuals and more (i.e. street names), sold them to people. Later that was acquired and it became popularized on a national scale.

Fountain drinks - Water fountains were a thing back then. But they were created with these cups that you pour into and share with everyone. You're then supposed to then replace that cup for the next person for sanitary purposes. Then they designed another type that shot water vertically straight up. People just put their mouths on them and drank from there. Was not sanitary as well... So the final iteration is what we have today. And incidents like the UCF "Watergate" incident caused regulations to give water to everyone, everywhere in public settings. Think about how it was back then, when alcohol was the cheapest thing to get access to and "water" was from the river. A.k.a. water was SUPER unsanitary. So people just drank alcohol for their liquids... not water. This water fountain was something that provided people who didn't want to be intoxicated all of the time a new primary option. People involved with the temperance movement (prohibition supporters) especially approved of this new adaption of water rather than alcohol. Isn't that kind of neat? Something we sort of ignore nowadays was treasured for people back then. Also, another cool thing that I learned: A guy named John Snow discovered that Cholera was caused by unsanitized water. He is known to be the "father of modern epidemiology."

Butterfly effect - The design of ballots in the U.S. is oddly still not that great... Prime example: 2000 Election - Bush v. Al Gore. Florida was the deciding state that would determine the presidential winner. Guess what? They had to do 2 manual recounts, and to be completely honest, it may have not been completely truthful. Not only were about 12,000 people not allowed to vote due to prejudice, but also the design of the voting ballot in certain places were poorly designed. In Palm Beach, FL county, there was something known as the "butterfly ballot." They required the voters to punch a hole into a paper to essentially vote for the person you wanted. Well for this paricular slip of paper, Bush was at the top and was clear when you wanted to vote for him. Al Gore, however, was down third in line. Some guy who wasn't very popular was sandwiched in between. According to the data after the election, that sandwiched guy was oddly high in the count. This was weird because it was probably a mistake in the interpretation of these ballots. These ballots were sometimes not able to fully be read by the machine (i.e. not fully punched through), and when they were recounted manually, they were easily misinterpreted by people as to who the person actually wanted to vote for. From the understanding of this ballot ticket, to the interpretation of it.... the butterfly ballot kind of sucks. The weird thing is that the U.S. has local systems for elections (i.e. every county decides what system they use). This means that bad design can happen anywhere since there's no standard. This is a serious problem, but to be completely honest it's not completely uncalled for. The amount of voting one could do is very different from county to county. So therefore when you want to vote for something that is particular to a state, the system would have to allow for this.

I'm telling you design is cool.
